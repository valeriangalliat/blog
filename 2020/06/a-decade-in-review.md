A decade in review
==================
September 23, 2020

~~We're halfway through 2020, and I'm about to turn 25. It seems like a
perfect time to do a checkpoint.~~

We're... a bit more than halfway through 2020, and I turned 25 a couple
of months ago. It seems like a perfect time to do a checkpoint.

The last time I did something like this was 5 years ago, when I was
[Looking back at 2015](../../2016/03/looking-back-at-2015.md). Again,
the value of this article is more for me to reflect about the past
decade, than for anyone else to read about, but if you want to know what
I've been up to, read on!

I've bean meaning to write this article since the beginning of 2020, and
that's about when I started writing the first draft, but I never managed
to finish that article until now. You could say I procrastinated a lot
on this one, but I did so by doing other productive things, so I guess
we can say it's some sort of prioritization.

2010, the tutorial level is over, time to start playing
-------------------------------------------------------

Reviewing this whole decade is quite a major thing for me since it's
essentially looking back at where my life *really* started.

I was 15, I was somewhat done growing up <small>(well not really, are we
ever done growing up anyways?)</small> and I was starting to do my own
things instead of just following my parents.

Essentially, before that, I was kind of on the passenger seat of my own
life. Like watching the introduction cinematic of that cool new game
that you just bought (or well, more accurately, got gifted), and then
doing the tutorial level. You're not really playing yet, and definitely
not achieving much, but you're *learning the essentials mechanics of the
gameplay*.

Life is a complicated game, and this was definitely a crucial step.
Though, at 15, I finally felt ready to *start playing*. On to the driver
seat, and let's pull that throttle!

At that point, my life revolves around two things; music and video games
(well mostly video games let's be honest here). While I spent 8 years of
my life learning to play classical piano, and another 3 learning the
classical acoustic guitar, what I really wanted since, pretty much
forever, was to **rock** on an electric guitar. And play drums, but
that's another story.

The first real decision I made for myself in life was actually the year
before, where I went all around my family to gather enough money (on top
of my little savings) to buy an electric guitar. I've been asking my
parents for one since a good 8 years, without success, and I finally
understood that I wouldn't get one until I can pay for it myself.

I wanted a black and white Fender Stratocaster, like Jimi Hendrix's.
Since I could definitely not afford one at that time, I went with the
next closest cheapest thing; a black and white Cort G250. I turned up
the gain all way up despite my guitar teacher constantly reminding me
that I didn't really need to put it all the way and my father telling me
I didn't sound clean. I didn't want to sound clean. I wanted *radical*
distortion. And that little practice amp that I got with it actually did
deliver solid distortion. And that made me *happy*.

2011, FUNK, graphic design, YouTube and programming
---------------------------------------------------

Playing hard rock on my electric guitar was fun, but you know what's
also fun? GTA IV. That's actually where I spent most of my time,
especially online, and GTA IV online had a much, much bigger (and
positive) impact in my life that one would a expect a video game to
have.

I didn't realize it back then, but we can definitely say that 2011 was a
fucking intense year for me, and it kicked off a flying start to my
life.

### FUNK

Online, I meet this guy, Chemse. He's a fine driver racing in his
Banshee (the GTA equivalent of a Dodge Viper) and he's got a heavy
finger on the trigger of his Uzi to shoot punk ass motherfuckers. And
even more importantly when we ride together he blasts
[Space 103.2](https://gta.fandom.com/wiki/Space_103.2),
[K109](https://gta.fandom.com/wiki/K109_The_Studio)
and [IF99](https://gta.fandom.com/wiki/International_Funk_99)
and we jam to the tight grooves of those legendary funk beats.

That's where I realize I can actually use the clean channel of my amp.
And hell, didn't I know that the 3 single coil pickups of that Cort do
sound terribly awesome for funk.

Since this day I never stopped playing and listening to funk. I think I
spent the next 5 years nearly exclusively trying to listen to all the
tunes that were ever produced on earth that sound more or less like
funk (and I still am trying).

### Graphic design

One thing that I was always impressed with on the Internet was what
people were able to do with Photoshop, and graphic design in general
(but especially Photoshop).

I was dedicated to learn how to do this. So I drowned myself in
*hundreds* of hours of tutorials about Photoshop, but also Illustrator,
InDesign and even god fucking damned Adobe Fireworks which turned to be
one of the most useless things I purposely chose to learn (I definitely
lost even more time in my childhood learning things that I didn't
willingly meant to learn but that's for another article).

And since I wanted some *concrete* ideas to work with in order to
practice and learn, I was constantly browsing forums, looking for people
who wanted help to make random website and forum banners, YouTube
channel backgrounds (that was a thing back then), avatars, logos,
profile pictures and so on. I was doing it mostly for free and that gave
me enough *meat* to play with and learn those precious skills.

Until I got *decent* enough that I could actually ask for *some* money
in return. And that was my first *life-changing* realizations; I could
solve people's problems with skills that I *enjoyed* practicing and they
were willing to pay me <small>(very little)</small> for it.

### YouTube

That's also the year where I started my
[YouTube channel](https://www.youtube.com/FunkyVal). My thing back then
was graphic and other realism mods for GTA IV, and I loved to showcase
them in videos. GTA IV had a very solid built-in video editor to record
gameplay sequences and edit the camera angles and moves after the fact,
and organize shots across sequences, which gave pretty impressive
results and taught me quite a lot about video editing.

Making videos was pretty captivating, up to a point where I was spending
more time working on the videos than actually playing the game.

### Programming

You know what? Online, I meet this guy. Yeah, this other guy, Adrien (I
think). He usually goes by `Xx<insert some bird name here>xX`.

He's not really into funk, but he's still a cool guy and we play a lot
together, pretty much everyday. And some day, he disappears, without
saying anything. I'm puzzled, but well, I keep playing.

A month or two later, the dude comes back like it's business as usual.

I'm like bro, what the fuck.

He's like bro, I'm learning to make websites, it's pretty sick, you use
HTML for that and then you can add CSS and style the hell out of it.

I'm like dawg that sounds dope, where u learning that shit?

He's learning that shit on Site du Zéro, which was a French website that
centralized free tutorials about many, many things, including web
development and programming in general. The idea was to teach things
from the most absolute beginner level so that anybody could learn
without any prerequisites. And it was fucking awesome.

It's now called OpenClassrooms, and I don't know if it's still as
awesome as it was back at the Site du Zéro era, but I sure hope so!

Turned out that HTML was kinda hard for me back then and I quickly gave
up and went back to playing GTA. Until I tried a second time 6 months
later because I now had a need for a website; I wanted to showcase my
pack of graphic and realism mods!

As always with motivating myself to achieve something, the key is that I
need to, well, actually *need* that thing, *concretely*, and *right
fucking now*.

This now was the case for making websites and let me tell you that this
HTML tutorial instantly became far easier. Don't get me wrong, it was
actually hard for me back then and I struggled my ass on it for weeks,
but it was easier *to handle the hardness*, to the point where I would
actually be fine to struggle through it.

2012, big holy pile of fucking mess year
----------------------------------------

Did I say earlier that 2011 was an intense year in my life? Well, that's
because I didn't experience 2012 just yet.

### Part 1, FUNK, but really, programming

Learning HTML and CSS happened near the end of 2011, and at that point I
was mostly doing just that. This was an endless learning highway and
this was *deeply* exciting to me. I loved it, and I could just *learn*
more to keep loving it. I would *never* get bored as there was an
overwhelming infinity of things to learn.

HTML and CSS quickly lead me to JavaScript in the browser, which
required me to learn, essentially, *programming*.

HTML and CSS made my life easy enough that for what I was doing, I
didn't need to care about conditions, loops and data structures, among
(many) other things. Once I introduced JavaScript, those (many, many)
things were now on the plate. And you can be sure I stuffed my face with
that plate and I couldn't have enough.

Then I added PHP, because PHP was kinda the thing back then especially
for my kind of profile (knows nothing about servers and wants to run
shit on some kind of server).

I was starting to do somewhat decent work with websites and I was still
looking for some challenges on forums to get opportunities to learn even
more. That's where I make a friend, Octavio, who runs a web design
company in Belgium, and he sure have some challenges for me.

I help him on more and more projects to the point where he tells me
dude, I need to pay you for what you're doing. And he paid me more than
I would have ever dared to ask at that time (even though looking at it
now it wasn't much, that meant **a lot** to me back then).

That's my second *life-changing* realization, which is essentially a
continuation of the first one; not only I can help people by doing
something that I love and make *some* money, but that *some* is becoming
substantial (in context), and that's actually a game changer. This shit
is actually going somewhere, and it's getting there faster than I would
have expected.

And I realized that only because Octavio was kind enough to pay me what
he thought that I *deserved* rather than what I was asking for (having
no idea what my "work", that didn't even really feel like work back
then, was worth on the market), and I'll forever be thankful for that.

I later discovered open-source where I could help *way more people*
doing what I love while not getting paid at all.

### Part 2, time for the actual fucking mess

We're at the end of the spring, I've been crushing programming like
never before, and I realize it's getting *somewhere*. But guess what's
not going *anywhere*? Yeah. School.

At that time I'm in the middle of the French equivalent of high school.
Well, in theory. In practice I'm not really going anymore. I already
don't have enough time to learn about PostgreSQL window functions, let
alone learning about French literature, history and random mathematical
concepts that don't help me to vertically center `div`s in my web pages.
I didn't give a remote fuck about ordering beer and fish and chips in
English (even though this actually turned out to be a pretty crucial
skill to learn), I cared about being able to read the `awk` man page
without having to Google Translate every other word, and school wasn't
helping me much there.

As one can expect, this kind of starts to cause issues, my grades are
flirting with the absolute zero, as is the number of fucks I give about
that fact. At that point I'm only bothering to go to school for the
classes where we worked on computers so that I could work on my own shit
instead, but teachers ended up noticing that and started kicking me out
of those classes because I was doing JavaScript instead learning how to
turn on and off Microsoft Windows.

As far as I can remember, I kept going to French, English and history
classes because the teachers were actually really nice and captivating,
and even though I didn't remember much of what they said, that was an
enjoyable experience. Turns out that I don't really remember anything
that I don't actually *do*, as in, *physically*. That's why I never
remember anything about movies that I watched, even the ones I loved,
and for that matter, this kind of classes too, as good were they.

Since that was not enough, I get convoked to the principal's office
where he tells me that my grades and presence is terrible and that I
need to be more hardworking. I explain him that I'm hardworking, just on
something different. What are my options? I ask. According to him, my
only option is to put that away for the next two years and focus *only*
on school until I graduate. Then I'll do whatever I want.

I was not satisfied with that proposition. Though I didn't know if he
was right or wrong about the fact that my only option to be a programmer
was to finish school and graduate first, that didn't *feel* right.  I
wasn't even learning shit about programming in school, so how would that
help me achieving that goal? Since that was not an acceptable solution
to me, I challenged it. I took the bet that I could be a programmer
*without* finishing school and graduating.

### Part 3, stirring the mess a bit more

I sent mails to a ton of different software companies and web agencies
in my city. The very few ones to get an answer are rejections.

That hurts.

But I'm 17 and I don't have any diploma nor experience. What did I
expect? Everybody was like, who the fuck that kid that claims to code
PHP and Node.js?

That shit won't work. My Belgian friend offers me to come to Belgium and
work for him, but I'm not really ready for that. One thing at a time.
Today, I'm still curious what my life would have been like if I accepted
that offer, but that can only be fiction at that point.

My next option was to go to a graphic design, 3D and programming school
that I happened to have done a two weeks internship the year before.
They know I can code, and I know that they have a work-study program
that would allow me to work 3 days a week, and that companies would be
more open to hiring me in that context (especially because it legally
allows them to pay me like garbage, and the government aids are paying
for the vast majority of that garbage anyways).

But guess what I hear? Something that I heard before, and something that
I quite didn't like. I needed to graduate first before being able to
apply for the work-study program.

Fair enough.

At that point the school thing is just fully out of control and there's
no fucking going back. This is not anymore an option, if it ever was. So
I officially drop out. Bye motherfuckers, have a great life.

My father tells me that I can't just stay at home on my computer,
whether I'm using that time for programming or not, I needed to get a
job, whatever it is.

That also hurts. But as stubborn as I am back then, I can't really argue
against that. So I register on Pôle emploi, the government's employment
agency, and apply to anything that don't require a diploma.

I end up being hired as an apprentice bartender, or more specifically,
for a pre-apprentice bartender internship. Essentially a two months
screening period to make sure that I can put stuff in and out of the
dishwasher and pour properly dosed panaché (beer and lemonade) and
monaco (panaché with extra grenadine syrup). And by *properly dosed*, I
don't mean dosed in the way that tastes the best (hell, I'm not even
legal to drink), but dosed as per the boss' rules, aka the *cheapest*
way (and no matter how cheap Heineken tastes, it's still not as cheap as
lemonade, and hell, don't use the Leffe tap for a monaco). Needless to
say this internship wasn't paid whatsoever.

### Part 4, solving the mess, finally

The two months reveals that I can properly serve watered-down red fruit
flavored beers to drunk HEC students and Côtes du Rhône pitchers to
businessmen having lunch on the terrace, and I get offered an
apprenticeship, which is essentially an upgrade where instead of working
full time for free, I would be legally working for a misery.

I kindly decline, and go back to the programming school where I'm like
hey BTW I dropped out of school. You know I can program. Are you sure
you can't help me with that?

They're like, right, listen. We have that "gateway" thingy that's like,
kinda there, but that nobody really knows about or uses. It states that
even without having a high school diploma, you could start the
work-study program by passing a technical test instead.

Music to my ears.

That "exception" was actually designed for people in professional
reconversion that started working 40+ years ago where it wasn't as
common to even go to high school. But hey, nothing states that a 17
years old can't use that as well.

I successfully proved that I could write HTML and CSS for a
basic website with a header, footer and sidebar (that was "the test"),
meaning that I successfully dropped out of (not programming) school to
go to (programming) school instead.

Win.

The biggest mess-solving part was done. It took a couple more months in
order for me to find the "work" part of the work-study program. I first
got an offer from a Node.js company, but they called me a couple weeks
later being like yo, BTW we found that other dude who's like, 25 and we
can pay him the same as u so well, it's not that we don't like you, you
know, but kbye.

That hurts, one more time.

Finally, a week or so before I was supposed to start the work-study
thing, I get hired in a web agency that makes e-commerce websites and a
custom analytics and management platform in PHP.

Finding a job ended up being a struggle even after I secured the school,
but at least was *doable* (without me moving to another country). I
started school and work somewhere in September, and the rest is history.

2013, servers, Unix severs
--------------------------

I kept writing more and more PHP, and my use case for PHP involved
servers. Web servers at first, but really, after some time, just
*servers*. Servers that communicate with other servers. Database
servers. Mail servers. Server servers that serve virtualised servers,
and nowadays serverless "servers" that still serve shit and still runs
on actual fucking servers, but at least someone else deals with the
server and that's nice because I can drink beers instead of managing
servers, but I digress.

Servers quickly introduced me to Linux, or more specifically, GNU/Linux,
but eventually BSD and anything that looks in a way or another like an
Unix system.

And holy fucking shit that part was hell for me to learn. Some kind of
hell that I loved like nothing else.

Soon enough I wasn't paying much attention to the HTML class (remember
how I was making money from that two years ago) and I started blasting
funk through my headphones in class to compile Gentoo, fix `pacman -Syu`
and Git rebase conflicts, fork OpenSMTPD to make it work on NixOS,
configure Vim on my OpenBSD dual boot, and figure out how to enable IPv6
on my router to access my FreeBSD home server from school without caring
about the dynamically allocated IPv4 address that my ISP provided.

This time the teacher didn't mind; I was passing the exams, and most
importantly, I was happily helping everybody, whether it was with fixing
the obscure bugs you encounter when trying to setup an Apache server on
Windows for the first time, configuring phpMyAdmin to allow running that
SQL file that's too big for the default settings, or introducing the
HTML5, CSS3 and ES5 "novelties" to the class. That was a win for
everybody.

At that point everything is pretty stable and chill.

2015 ended up being quite an intense year as well, and I've already

### Programming

The first part of the year is just a continuation of 2014. I get into

When the summer arrives, I realize I accumulated 3 months worth of days

### Mountains

At the point where I was basically useless and doing nothing meaningful
of my days, I figured I would try something else, and started biking.

This was pretty painful as I basically didn't do any kind of exercise
for the past 5 years, but nevertheless, I pushed through it. I started
with small distances that would get me super exhausted and thirsty, but
by biking nearly everyday, I noticed I was going farther and farther
while being less out of breath, up to the point where I'm doing, 20, 40

That's also when I start to take my car to go hiking, by myself, or when

Leg day was actually my only kind of day back then if I was to do some
kind of at all, as I was was only biking and hiking, so we were on a
more even ground when climbing mountains together.

I spent the whole summer in the mountains, surrounded by chamois and icy
lakes, and totaling 20K meters of cumulated elevation, which actually
turned out to be the most I ever hiked in a year to date, since I've

### Canada

I'm graduating from programming school at the end of the summer, which
gives me some kind of
master-thingy-that's-not-really-a-master-but-equivalent certificate.

I heard earlier in the year about the Working Holiday Visa program, and
that sounded like a good thing for me, since I was turning 20 and was
eager to go abroad.

Australia is my first choice. That said I'm at a point where I'm just
kick starting my programming career, and they don't allow to work
qualified jobs on a Working Holiday Visa (and you can't have the same

Canada is my second choice, and especially, Quebec, since they speak
French, which would make my life a lot easier in the beginning, as I'm
decent at reading (and to some extent, writing) technical English, but
I'm not that good at understanding oral English and I pretty much can't
talk whatsoever.

That's why earlier that year I applied for a Canadian Working Holiday
Visa and was lucky enough to get one. Back then it was a first come
first served kind of thing, and there was far more people coming than
could actually being served.

I started looking for jobs in Québec at the end of the summer, and I was
impressed at how fast and easy it was to get offers, and that, just
based on my concrete programming skills (I didn't even have my diploma
yet and nobody in Canada gave a fuck whatsoever about some random
European programming certificate anyways).

The second thing that impressed me was the salary, which was
significantly more than what I would have hoped to get as a full time
developer in France at that time.

In September, I fly to Montreal and start at Busbud, where I'm warmly
welcomed by an amazingly friendly team, and quickly make a lot of
friends even though I'm still pretty shy at that point (alcohol might or
might not have helped with that). We created deep connections that went
much beyond being work colleagues, many of those who are still super
close friends of mine today even though most of us are not working there
anymore.

2017, photography, new rope, new guitar, new place
--------------------------------------------------

### Photography

2017 is the year where I get my first "real" camera (as in, not my phone
camera), a Panasonic LX100. It's a compact camera, but it's one of the
rare compact cameras to have a Micro Four Thirds sensor, that you would
usually find in mirrorless cameras.

It's a stunning camera for its size and weight, it comes with an
incredible lens which versatility is hardly attained in other cameras
without having to switch lens, and I've always been amazed by the
results.

For sure you can get better performance, even sharper and higher
resolution images, more bokeh, longer focal length and so on with DSLRs
and a couple of high quality lenses, but you also need to be willing to
carry that extra weight and spending the extra time.

As much as I love photography, I'm not *that* dedicated, and the LX100
allows me to take photos in a flash without being in my way. I can be
hiking around with friends who don't care about photography, and still
take quick and decent pictures on the way without slowing the group
down, having to setup a tripod, switch lenses, play with filters and so
on, which is incredibly valuable to me, and I'm willing to take that
trade-off on image quality and creative options.

You can find many of the pictures I've taken since then on my
[photography website](https://photography.codejam.info/).

### Climbing, climbing, lots of climbing

I kick off the year with an ice climbing course which was an awesome
experience! Didn't love it to the point I want to invest a couple
thousands of dollars in gear to do it all winter, but was definitely
nice to try it out. But I love skiing way too much to leave room for any
other winter sport really.

At that point, I'm going to the block climbing gym once or twice a week,
during the lunch break (I work just next to the gym). I meet a guy
there, Gabriel. We're about the same level and he's always *beta
spraying* me which I really appreciated, and we start to climb a lot
together. He's climbing every single day and pushes me to do so as well,
so I get a unlimited membership and start going 5 days a week.

Needless to say my climbing improved drastically since I did that, and
even though I was pretty much *constantly* sore and 2 days of break
during the week-end wasn't enough to fully recover (especially I would
often go climbing outside one day of the week-end), I just got used to
climbing sore all the time.

This is probably not the best strategy, and I would likely have gotten
even better results giving enough rest time to my muscles. That said,
making it a daily routine that I don't even think about anymore played
a key role in me being consistent with climbing, and I believe that
alone made it worth it.

Climbing energized me like crazy and gave ma a deep break in the middle
of the day, and that made me more efficient at my job even
though I was spending more time on my break than usual.
